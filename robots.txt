<div class="manifesto-hero">
  <div class="sec-eye">Why Reframe Aging</div>
  <h1>Aging is inevitable.<br><em>Loss of independence is not.</em></h1>
  <p class="lead">A clinical manifesto on physical capacity, the difference between lifespan and healthspan, and what it means to age on your own terms.</p>
</div>

<div class="manifesto-body">
  <div style="max-width:680px;">

    <div class="principle">
      <div class="principle-num">I</div>
      <h2>The problem with how we think about aging.</h2>
      <p>Most conversations about aging begin with decline. The imagery is predictable: slower, frailer, more dependent. Medicine has largely reinforced this narrative &mdash; engaging with aging primarily after something has gone wrong, after the fall, after the fracture, after the diagnosis.</p>
      <p>This is not a criticism of the clinicians involved. It is a description of how the system is structured. We have built an extraordinary infrastructure for tertiary care &mdash; for treating the consequences of decline &mdash; and a comparatively modest one for preventing it.</p>
      <p>The result is that most people encounter the healthcare system as aging adults at exactly the wrong time: after the reserve they needed has already been lost.</p>
      <blockquote><p>&ldquo;There comes a point where we need to stop just pulling people out of the river. We need to go upstream and find out why they&rsquo;re falling in.&rdquo;<br><span style="font-size:0.8rem;font-style:normal;color:var(--ink-muted);font-family:var(--fb);">&mdash; Desmond Tutu, as cited in Peter Attia, Outlive</span></p></blockquote>
      <p>Reframe Aging exists upstream.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="principle">
      <div class="principle-num">II</div>
      <h2>The Capacity Principle.</h2>
      <p>The difference between thriving and struggling as we age is rarely age itself. It is capacity.</p>
      <p>Capacity is the physical reserve available to meet life&rsquo;s demands. Walking through an airport, getting up from the floor, carrying groceries, traveling with grandchildren, navigating uneven terrain, recovering from illness &mdash; all of these require physical reserve. That reserve either grows deliberately over time, or erodes quietly without consistent investment.</p>
      <p>When capacity exceeds demand, people flourish. When demand exceeds capacity, a predictable cascade begins: reduced activity, loss of confidence, social withdrawal, functional decline, and ultimately dependency.</p>
      <p>What makes this principle actionable is that capacity is largely modifiable. The American Geriatrics Society, the World Health Organization, and decades of longitudinal research are consistent on this point: disease and disability are not inevitable outcomes of aging. The experience of aging is profoundly shaped by how much physical reserve a person has built &mdash; and how early they started building it.</p>
      <blockquote><p>&ldquo;The goal is not simply to live longer. It is to maintain the capacity to continue living well.&rdquo;</p></blockquote>
    </div>

    <div class="principle">
      <div class="principle-num">III</div>
      <h2>Lifespan and healthspan are not the same thing.</h2>
      <p>Lifespan is how long you live. Healthspan is how long you live well &mdash; with the physical capacity, cognitive vitality, and functional independence to do the things that give life meaning.</p>
      <p>Modern medicine has become exceptionally good at extending lifespan. It has been slower to prioritize healthspan. The result, for many people, is an extended period at the end of life characterized by declining function, increasing dependency, and diminishing quality of experience.</p>
      <p>The goal of Reframe Aging is to compress that period &mdash; to extend the years of genuine capability and compress the period of decline at the end. Not by avoiding aging, but by building enough reserve that aging has less to take.</p>
      <p>Peter Attia describes this as training for the &ldquo;Centenarian Decathlon&rdquo; &mdash; identifying the specific physical tasks that will matter most to you in your eighth and ninth decades, and systematically building the capacity to perform them. To pick up a grandchild at 80, you need to be able to lift considerably more today. To hike a trail at 75, your aerobic capacity needs to be higher now than most people realize. The margin you build in your 40s and 50s is the independence you keep in your 80s.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="principle">
      <div class="principle-num">IV</div>
      <h2>Exercise is the most powerful longevity drug available.</h2>
      <p>No pharmaceutical intervention comes close to the magnitude and breadth of benefit that consistent physical activity provides across the lifespan. This is not a wellness platitude &mdash; it is one of the most robust findings in the scientific literature.</p>
      <p>Regular physical activity reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease, metabolic dysfunction, cognitive decline, and certain cancers. It preserves muscle mass, bone density, balance, and reaction time. It supports sleep, mood, and psychological resilience. It is, in the words of the evidence, the single most effective intervention available for extending both lifespan and healthspan.</p>
      <p>And yet most adults are not meaningfully engaged with it &mdash; not because they are lazy, but because they have not been given a clear framework for what to do, why it matters, and how to sustain it across decades rather than weeks.</p>
      <p>That framework is what Reframe Aging aims to provide.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="principle">
      <div class="principle-num">V</div>
      <h2>Healthy aging is multidimensional.</h2>
      <p>Physical capacity is necessary but not sufficient. How people age is shaped by a broader ecosystem of factors: psychological resilience, cognitive engagement, social connection, and the environments in which they live.</p>
      <p>Research on the world&rsquo;s longest-lived populations &mdash; the Blue Zones studied by Dan Buettner, the centenarian cohorts analyzed by longevity researchers &mdash; consistently identifies social integration, purpose, and meaning as defining features of longevity. These are not soft variables. They are biological ones, shaping stress hormones, immune function, and the fundamental trajectory of aging.</p>
      <p>Physical therapy done well addresses movement. Reframe Aging aims to address the person moving &mdash; their beliefs, their confidence, their goals, and the life they are building the capacity to live.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="principle">
      <div class="principle-num">VI</div>
      <h2>What proactive care actually looks like.</h2>
      <p>The Geriatric Functional Milestones &mdash; a clinical framework I co-authored with colleagues &mdash; proposes that routine assessment of movement through the lifespan can identify suboptimal patterns before they become pathological. Walking without an assistive device. Rising from a chair. Getting on and off the floor. Standing on one leg. These are not just physical tasks &mdash; they are proxies for the physical reserve required for independence.</p>
      <p>A meaningful decline in any of these in a six-month window is not a reason for alarm. It is an invitation for assessment. Not because decline is inevitable, but because early identification changes the trajectory. Most of what looks like irreversible aging, addressed early enough, is not.</p>
      <p>This is the work: identifying the gap between where someone is and where they need to be &mdash; not just for today, but for the decades ahead &mdash; and building the bridge between those two points, deliberately and with evidence behind every step.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="principle">
      <div class="principle-num">VII</div>
      <h2>A note on the name.</h2>
      <p>Reframe Aging is a deliberate choice of words. Not anti-aging, not reverse aging, not defeat aging &mdash; but reframe it.</p>
      <p>Aging is not the enemy. It is not something to be fought or feared or denied. It is the context in which a human life unfolds. The question is not whether we age, but how &mdash; with how much capability, how much engagement, how much on our own terms.</p>
      <p>The cultural default is a narrative of decline: that aging means slowing down, losing ground, gradually becoming less. Reframe Aging challenges that default &mdash; not with false optimism, but with clinical evidence that most people are capable of far more than the default assumes, for far longer than the default suggests, if they invest in the right things early enough.</p>
      <p>That is the work. And it starts now, wherever you are.</p>
    </div>
